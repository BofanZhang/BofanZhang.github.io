Ancient Egyptians 3100 B.C.E

The ancient Egyptians were an advanced civilization that was established along the Nile River. While the Egyptians made many important contributions to human civilization, their influence on Europeans was indirect and happened more through the general spread of Egyptian culture and knowledge. Some of the ways Egyptians contributed to the development of rational thinking in Europe were through their early medicine practices, their own writing system, and their influence on Greek knowledge. In ancient Egypt, medical practices were a combination of religious beliefs, empirical knowledge, and practical applications. As the Egyptians had an extensive knowledge of the properties of herbs and botanicals, herbal remedies were a common practice in Egyptian medicinal treatments. They were also often accompanied by religious rituals and prayers for full effect as they believed that all diseases had both a natural and a supernatural cause. In addition, they were also capable of performing basic surgical procedures such as treating wounds and broken bones and performing dental procedures. This knowledge contributed to the progression of medicine and would spread across neighbouring countries, eventually contributing to the rational approaches future European countries would take toward health practices. The ancient Egyptians developed one of the first writing systems in history, the hieroglyphics. This made it possible for complex ideas to be conveyed through written language for the first time. The hieroglyphics would become a model for future writing systems, laying the foundation for the development of human communication, and indirectly creating the groundwork for rational discourse. Significant Greek scholars whose work was heavily studied by future European scholars, such as Herodotus and Pythagoras would travel to Egypt to exchange ideas which would eventually lead to Egypt's indirect transmission of ideas. In addition, Greek mathematicians were heavily influenced by Egyptian mathematics, particularly geometry, and would develop their works by building on earlier Egyptian knowledge. Hence, Egyptian knowledge would become the intellectual foundation of many works that contributed to the development of rational thinking in Europe.

The Ancient Hebrews 3761 B.C.E

The ancient Hebrews are most well known for their religion Judaism and the religions that stemmed out of it, Christianity and Islam. According to the Hebrew Bible, the Hebrew people were formed in 1900 BCE when their first patriarch Abraham, led them out of Mesopotamia to become wandering herders. It was during this time that Abraham agreed to the Covenant, a promise with the God Yahweh, which states that Yahweh shall grant the Hebrews a land of peace and prosperity in exchange for their devotion and worship. However, while the Hebrews were devoted to Yahweh, they would still believe in the existence of other gods during this time as it wasn't until several centuries later that they would condemn polytheism. It was during a time now remembered as the Prophetic Movement where prophets would denounce the existence of other gods and call for the Hebrews to exclusively worship Yahweh which would lead the Hebrews to true monotheism. This made the Hebrews one of the first civilizations to practice monotheism, laying the foundation for people to begin questioning polytheistic beliefs and superstitions. Sometime after establishing the Covenant, their leader at the time, Moses, would modify it into the 10 Commandments which included specific rules on behaviour the Hebrews were to abide by for guarantee of prosperity from Yahweh. This differed from the religions at the time where the gods would be indifferent to the ethical conduct of its people so long as the proper rituals and prayers were performed. An important difference between Yahweh and the other anthropomorphic gods of the time was that Yahweh only punished people who defied him. The concept of having one rational god encouraged the Hebrews to seek understanding through reason. Many religious Hebrew texts emphasized the importance of moral and ethical responsibilities and promoted critical thinking. This would heavily influence future European philosophers to seek a rational outlook. In addition, Hebrew teaching would often promote justice, fairness, and the idea of individual rights which European societies would eventually use to form the framework of their legal systems.

The Ancient Greeks 800 B.C.E - 146 B.C.E

During the Hellenic Age, the ancient Greeks significantly contributed to the development of rational thought in Europe through their contributions in philosophy, science, literature, and democracy. Ancient Greek philosophy placed great emphasis on reason and inquiry, promoting the idea of having a logical observation of the world. Philosophers such as Socrates, Aristotle, and Plato encouraged people to seek knowledge through critical thinking and logical reasoning rather than attributing it to the whim of gods. This would form the basis of Western philosophy and set the foundation for rational thought in Europe. Due to the philosophical encouragement of scientific inquiry, early Greek scientists were able to make significant contributions to various fields through their empirical observations. Many of these contributions such as the first astronomical models, the concept of mathematical proofs, and medical treatises would form the groundwork of today’s modern sciences. Even through literature, Greek writers such as Homer, Aeschylus, and Sophocles promoted critical thinking and rational reflection on human nature and ethical and social issues. They did this through their works which explored the complexity of human emotions, moral dilemmas, and social and political issues of their time. The flourishing city-state of Athens became one of the first societies to experiment with direct democracy where male citizens were able to participate directly in the decision-making processes of the state. This democracy's governance encouraged making laws based on reason and collective interests, valuing the voice of individuals. This democratic government represented a significant step towards the concept of citizen participation. The legacy of the Hellenistic Age continues to have a lasting influence on Western civilization. These ideas of the Ancient Greeks heavily influenced the Romans, were revived and explored by the Europeans during the Renaissance, and significantly contributed to the Enlightenment.

The Romans 753 B.C.E

The ancient Romans were a civilization originating from the city of Rome in Italy. Their history was divided into three periods, the kingdom, the Roman Republic, and the Roman Empire. Not much is known about the kingdom era but according to legends, it was during this time period when Rome was founded by Romulus and Remus and was ruled as a monarchy. The Roman Republic was formed when the monarchy was overthrown and replaced by a representative democracy. New political offices and institutes were created along with a system that ensured the elected officials would be kept in check. Eventually, the Roman Republic would turn into the Roman Empire and become an absolute monarchy under the governance of Augustus, the first Roman Emperor. It was during this time that Rome would reach its peak and enter a state of relative peace and stability, allowing for the rise in cultural, economic, and architectural developments. The ancient Romans significantly contributed to Europe's transition towards a more rational outlook through their legal system and governance, historical inquiry, and spread of ideas. The Roman Republic established a complex legal system that emphasized rational principles such as equality before the law, consistency, and the rights of citizens. They created a set of laws named the Twelve Tables that would later become the basis of European legal systems. In addition, the Roman Republic also had a democratic government where all the officials who governed the state were elected and were kept in line by a system of checks and balances. This would create the foundation for how democratic political systems would be formed in modern societies. Roman historians approached history with a rational mindset, acknowledging the importance of examining the sources, evaluating the evidence, and interpreting the historical events. In addition, historians would also seek out primary sources such as official documents and eyewitness accounts to create a credible narrative. Influenced by Greek teachings, the Romans aimed to provide a balanced perspective that considered the different perspectives and analyzed the causes and effects of each event. This systematic approach to historical inquiry encouraged future historians to rationally analyze historical events, contributing to the development of historiography. Due to the Romans' vast empire, they were able to exchange ideas and cultures across regions. Including rational concepts which caused a broader acceptance of rational principles, contributing to the spread of rational thought and outlook in Europe.

The Renaissance 1300s

The period that moved into the age of enlightenment, the Renaissance was a movement by writers, philosophers, scientists, and poets to embrace reason over faith. Many people all over europe began to question the church's and monarch's legitimacy, and unrest grew into critical documents and writings that are still used today. People like Thomas Hobbes, Voltaire, and Rousseau, began to write in opposition or at least better management of the Monarch's reign. This era is probably one of the biggest increases in reason there is, suddenly jumping from entirely faith to slightly more reason than faith in the average populace. Many ideas and theories are still held from this era, and those that don't are still the building blocks for modern science today.

The Reformation 1517

The Reformation was a significant religious and social movement that occurred in the 16th century, primarily in Germany where the famous figure Martin Luther, started the call for change. It aimed to reform the Roman Catholic Church which had overwhelming power, which sometimes even rivalled the king. Martin Luther, is credited with starting the reformation by nailing his 95 theses to a church door in Germany. This would be a huge movement against the church that would later spread towards England and further west to the Americas. The 95 theses called for free access to the bible, as many at the time were written in Latin, which only “educated” clergy could read. This would allow the average commoner to gain access to the bible. Additionally, the reformation challenged the idea of one single religion by allowing the idea of Religious Pluralism which opened the world up to Protestants and many of its variations. Altogether, the Reformation was more towards reasoning however, it still contained the idea that religion was important making it a inbetween of Age of Reason and Age of Religion

The Scientific Revolution 1543 - 1687

The scientific revolution was a period of significant change in the scientific method and reasoning. It took place during the 16th and 17th centuries. It replaced the old scientific view on natural sciences which was based on the Ancient Greeks which had dominated the field for around 2000 years. During this time, people began to ask more questions about the world around them which led to more answers as people began to find answers to their questions. While seeking for answers, many began to use the same method inorder to get similar answers, which led to the development of the scientific method which we still use today.

The Scientific Revolution challenged traditional Christian beliefs about the origins of the universe and the natural world, leading to a conflict between Science and Religion. However, it is important to remember that religion and science can exist at the same time and that one is not “better” than the other. For example, Nicolaus Copernicus served the Catholic Church as an official but still had the freedom to conduct scientific research at the same time which led him to create the heliocentric system. While religion played a role in shaping scientific thought, it was reason and physical evidence that drove the scientific revolution.

One significant figure is Montesquieu. He suggests that there should be a separation of the Church and state, suggesting that we should have levels of government. The separation of government is still seen today in modern representative democracies around the world. Hobbes believed that all humans were born evil, while opposite to him, Rousseau believed that all humans are born good and that the environment eventually corrupts them. However in both cases, they both push for a social contract. A social contract is like a constitution, where the citizens get rights and are protected by the ones who they serve, ie the king/queen. While Locke believed people are born like a blank slate (Tabula Rasa) he is more known for his idea of natural rights which defines that all men have rights when they were born. This is now seen in many places such in the modern day; Declaration of Human Rights. We see other philosophers such as Adam Smith, The Father of Capitalism. Voltaire, who created the idea of free speech. And Diderot, the creator of the encyclopedie. All these men have the same in common. They all challenged the church and its religious teachings in some way or another. This age was a movement towards reasoning as many people began to question the church’s teachings. As people become more and more oppressed, many people begin to doubt, and with that, many people begin to demand for change which leads people thinking about a new way of life.
